Q & A

Question: What are your tolerances?

Answer:

Cutting is normally within 0.25mm.

Bending has many influences - we normally work on +/- 1mm. Critical bending tolerance needs to be communicated.

Key Bending Parameters

Minimum Bend Radius: This is the smallest possible radius for a bend without the material cracking or deforming undesirably.

  • Soft Materials like aluminium and mild steel: The minimum bend radius is often 1 times the material thickness.
  • Harder Materials like the other metals listed: A radius of 2 times the material thickness or more is generally required to prevent cracking.
  • General Rule: For materials between 2–6 mm thick, a radius of 1x thickness is often acceptable; for thicker materials, 2–3x is recommended.

Minimum Flange Length: This is the shortest possible length of material that can be successfully bent on a die.

  • General Rule: A common guideline suggests a minimum flange length of 4x the material thickness or 12mm, whichever is greater.

Z-Bends: A specific minimum X-measurement (distance from the bend to the edge) must be maintained to prevent tool collisions.

Feature Spacing: Features like holes or other bends need to be placed a sufficient distance from bend lines.

  • General Rule: Keep features at least 4 times the material thickness away from bend lines to avoid issues during manufacturing.

Curls: For more complex curls, the spacing from other features should be a minimum of the curl radius plus the sheet metal thickness.

Tolerances

  • Bend Angle Tolerance: A common tolerance for bend angles is ±1°.
  • Length Tolerance: A typical tolerance for bend length is ±0.20mm (0.010 inches) per bend.
  • ISO 2768: This international standard is a widely recognized reference for general tolerances in precision sheet metal manufacturing, encompassing linear, angular, and geometric tolerances.

Question: Do you have tips for effective bending?

Answer:

  • Avoid Acute Angles: Do not design for angles sharper than 130°.
  • Avoid Obtuse Angles: Do not design for angles greater than 5°.
  • Be Mindful of Successive Bends: Place bends as far apart as possible, as successive bends can be difficult to fit on the die and can complicate the manufacturing process.
  • Consider Material Hardness: The specific material and its yield strength are crucial factors in determining appropriate bend radii and overall feasibility.

Question: What metals do you offer?

Answer:

If you need something not in this list, please reach out to us.

Metals we offer:

Mild Steel: Mild steel (also called low-carbon steel) is a type of carbon steel that contains a relatively low percentage of carbon, typically 0.05% to 0.25%. Because of this low carbon content, it is ductile, malleable, and easy to work with, making it one of the most commonly used forms of steel in construction, manufacturing, and fabrication.

3CR12 Stainless Steel: 3CR12 is a corrosion-resistant stainless steel grade that falls within the utility ferritic stainless steels group. It was originally developed in South Africa by Columbus Stainless and is known as the world’s most commonly used 12% chromium utility stainless steel. It bridges the gap between carbon/mild steel and the more expensive austenitic stainless steels (like 304 and 316), offering a balance of corrosion resistance, strength, and cost-effectiveness.

430 Stainless Steel: 430 stainless steel is a straight chromium ferritic stainless steel that contains around 16–18% chromium and very little to no nickel. It is one of the most widely used ferritic stainless steels, valued for its good corrosion resistance, formability, and affordability compared to austenitic grades like 304.

304 Stainless Steel: 304 stainless steel is the most widely used stainless steel grade in the world. It belongs to the austenitic stainless steel family, known for its excellent corrosion resistance, durability, and formability. Because of its balanced properties and affordability, it is often called the “workhorse” of stainless steels.

316 Stainless Steel: 316 stainless steel is a premium austenitic stainless steel known for its exceptional corrosion resistance, especially in harsh environments. It is often called the “marine grade stainless steel” because it performs better than 304 in chloride-rich environments such as seawater and de-icing salts.

Aluminium 1050: Aluminium 1050 is a commercially pure aluminium grade (at least 99.5% aluminium content) and belongs to the 1xxx series of wrought aluminium alloys. It is one of the most commonly used “pure” aluminium grades, valued for its excellent corrosion resistance, high ductility, and very good thermal and electrical conductivity. It is a non-heat-treatable alloy, meaning its strength is achieved through cold working (work hardening) rather than heat treatment.

Question: How do I design a part?

Answer:

Refer to the How it works section for some guiding comments.

You need to have an idea of the shape of the part you need. You also need to know how large it should be. If it needs to fit into a certain space or be a part of a larger structure, you need to measure very carefully.

If you don’t have the skills or the time to produce a CAD model, send us a picture or a drawing of what you need, along with the critical dimensions, and thickness of the metal you plan to use.

If you need help with advice on how to get to a design to solve your particular problem, please get in touch.

If you are the type of person who likes to learn a new skill or if you want to play with the design until you are happy, we recommend that you learn CAD.

There is no standard way in which they work, so changing packages often requires some time.

Choose an option that has good online support and communities that provide content and forums.

Question: What are the different surface finishes?

Answer:

Refer to the Metals Available page for which finishes are available for which metals.

Hot rolled:

  • Surface finish: Rougher, with scale (oxide layer).
  • Dimensions: Less precise tolerances (can shrink while cooling).
  • Strength: Lower strength than cold rolled steel, but more ductile.
  • Cost: Cheaper to produce because it requires less processing.

Cold rolled:

  • Surface finish: Smooth, clean, often shiny.
  • Dimensions: Very precise thickness and shape control.
  • Strength: Higher tensile strength and hardness due to work hardening.
  • Cost: More expensive than hot rolled steel because of extra processing.

Galvanised:

Galvanized steel possesses excellent corrosion resistance due to its zinc coating, which acts as a protective barrier and sacrificial layer. It is also durable, cost-effective, and low-maintenance, with a long lifespan. Other key properties include its strength, weldability, formability, and recyclability.

2D (achieved through cold rolling, annealing, and pickling):

  • Dull, matte finish: It has a silvery-white, dull luster rather than a bright shine.
  • Non-reflective: Suitable for applications where light reflection is not a priority.
  • Smoothness: Smoother than a No. 1 finish but still a uniform, dull surface, though less smooth than a 2B finish.
  • Non-directional: The surface texture does not have a specific direction.
  • Corrosion Resistance: Chemically pickled, which enhances its corrosion resistance.

No.1 (achieved through hot rolling, annealing, and pickling):

  • Appearance: Rough, dull, off-white, and non-uniform; not polished for aesthetic appeal.
  • Cost-Effective: Less processing is required compared to other finishes.
  • Mechanical Properties: High strength and durability, suitable for demanding industrial applications.
  • Durability: Ideal for heavy-duty use where surface smoothness is not a priority.
  • High-Temperature Resistance: Suitable for high-temperature applications.

BA (annealing in a protective, oxygen-free atmosphere):

  • Appearance: Smooth, reflective, and bright, similar to a mirror; highly attractive visual quality.
  • Hygiene: Non-porous surface prevents bacterial growth, excellent for sanitary applications.
  • Corrosion Resistance: Enhanced chromium oxide layer provides superior protection.

2B (cold-rolling, annealing, and a light cold roll pass):

  • Surface Appearance: Smooth, bright, but not fully mirrored; hazy, semi-reflective.
  • Directional Pattern: Slight directional pattern from cold rolling.
  • Corrosion Resistance: Enhanced corrosion resistance typical of stainless steel.
  • Cost-Effectiveness: Economical choice for industrial and functional purposes.

2B PVC (2B finish with a removable plastic layer):

  • Material provided with a removable protective PVC (plastic) film.
  • The PVC film prevents scratches and damage during handling and transport.

PVC BA (BA finish with a removable plastic layer):

  • Material provided with a removable protective PVC (plastic) film.
  • The PVC film prevents scratches and damage during handling and transport.

No.4 PVC (created by fine abrasive belts with a removable plastic layer):

  • Appearance: Satin-like, non-reflective surface with a uniform brushed appearance.
  • Grain: Short, parallel lines that run along the length of the sheet.
  • Durability: Durable and often repairable by light abrasive pads, making it low-maintenance.
